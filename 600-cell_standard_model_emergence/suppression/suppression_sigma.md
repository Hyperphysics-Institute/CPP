# Holographic Entropy Suppression: σ = 120^{-d}

## Overview
The most fundamental and recurring suppression in Conscious Point Physics (CPP) is the holographic entropy factor σ = 120^{-d}, where d is the effective number of unbound dimensions in a given Zitterbewegung (ZBW) mode, projection, or interaction pathway. This factor quantifies how the finite information capacity of the 600-cell lattice (120 vertices per cell) dilutes organizational energy and coupling strength when modes become less bound or more diffusive.

## Geometric Origin
- The 600-cell lattice has exactly 120 vertices per fundamental cell—a topological invariant arising from its regular 4-polytopal structure (600 tetrahedral 4-cells, 120 vertices, icosahedral symmetry group).
- Each vertex functions as a Conscious Point (CP) processor with finite capacity to broadcast Space Stress Vector (SSV) gradients and Full Bit Strings (FBS).
- In fully bound orbital ZBW (e.g., electron spin), all interactions remain within the tightest lattice subgroup (d = 0 → σ = 1, no suppression).
- When modes include linear extras (down-type quarks) or fully unbound propagation (neutrinos), effective degrees of freedom increase, spreading DI-bits (informational units) across more lattice paths → exponential entropy increase → coupling suppression.
- The exponent -d reflects dimensional counting: each unbound dimension multiplies the number of accessible hyperedge pathways by a factor proportional to the coordination number, but holographically bounded by the vertex count → power-law 120^{-d}.

## Mathematical Form
σ = 120^{-d}  
Typical values:  
- d = 0 (bound orbital ZBW, charged lepton spin): σ = 1  
- d = 1 (linear qDP/hDP ZBW extras, down-type quarks): σ ≈ 8.33 × 10^{-3}  
- d = 3 (unbound orbital tetra ZBW, neutrinos): σ ≈ 5.8 × 10^{-7}

σ = 120^{-d} is not an adjustable parameter—it is the direct holographic consequence of the lattice having only 120 finite processors per cell. No other scale is introduced. The exponent d is determined unambiguously by the geometric character of each mode (bound vs. linear vs. unbound), as classified in the unified ZBW spectrum (Paper 2, Section 4).
