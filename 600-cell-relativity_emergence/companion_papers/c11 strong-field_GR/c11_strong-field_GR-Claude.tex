\section*{Plain Language Summary (draft)}
% ==================================================================

General relativity predicts that matter compressed beyond a critical
radius --- the Schwarzschild radius --- collapses into a black hole
with an event horizon: a surface from which nothing, not even light,
can escape.  At the very centre lies a singularity where the density
becomes infinite and the equations of GR break down.

Conscious Point Physics makes a different prediction.  In CPP, space
is not a continuous manifold but a discrete 600-cell lattice of Grid
Points, each broadcasting its local state once per Planck-time tick.
Two features of this lattice prevent the GR singularity from forming:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The \emph{CP Exclusion Rule} — no two Conscious Points can
  occupy the same Grid Point simultaneously.  This imposes a hard
  lower bound on physical density at the Planck scale, replacing the
  GR singularity with a Planck-scale core.

\item The \emph{PSR formula} — the local rate of time and the local
  speed of light are both determined by the Space Stress Vector field,
  which saturates rather than diverging as matter is compressed.
  This means the CPP metric has no coordinate singularity at the
  Schwarzschild radius.
\end{enumerate}

The CPP metric agrees with GR to better than 1\% everywhere that
current observations have been made (at distances $r > 10\,r_s$
from a black hole).  The predictions diverge only in the strong-field
regime --- within a few Schwarzschild radii of the object --- and at
the Planck scale near the core.  Both regimes are currently beyond
observational reach but may become testable with future
gravitational-wave observations.

\subsection{The SSV field from a point mass}

[Follows from companion~7 and Newtonian gravity companion~5.]

The total integrated SSV at radius $r$ from a point mass $M$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\SSV(r) \;=\; \frac{Mc^2}{l_P^2\,r}.
  \label{eq:DSSV}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Note on the 4$\pi$ factor.} The shell-broadcast formula
in companion~7 gives a \emph{per-steradian} SSV density
$Mc^2/(4\pi l_P^2 r)$.  The PSR formula responds to the
\emph{total} SSV magnitude at a GP, which is the integral over all
incoming shells: this restores the $4\pi$ and gives
Eq.~\eqref{eq:DSSV}.  The identification $k\Delta\SSV = GM/rc^2$
then holds exactly.

\subsection{The CPP coupling constant}

\begin{equation}
  k \;\equiv\; \frac{l_P^3}{E_P}
  \;=\; \frac{l_P^3}{m_P c^2}
  \;=\; \frac{G}{c^4}.
  \label{eq:k}
\end{equation}

Therefore $k\Delta\SSV = (G/c^4)\cdot(Mc^2/l_P^2 r)\cdot l_P^2
= GM/(c^2 r) = r_s/(2r)$, the standard compactness parameter.

\subsection{The PSR formula}

\begin{equation}
  \frac{\PSR_{\rm eff}}{l_P}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + k\,\Delta\SSV}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + GM/rc^2}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + r_s/2r}.
  \label{eq:PSR}
\end{equation}

\section{Horizon Structure: CPP vs.\ GR}
\label{sec:horizon}
% ==================================================================

\subsection{No Schwarzschild horizon in CPP}

The Schwarzschild horizon at $r = r_s$ is defined by $g_{tt} = 0$.
In the CPP metric:
\begin{equation}
  g_{tt}^{\rm CPP}(r_s) \;=\; \frac{1}{(1+1/2)^2}
  \;=\; \frac{4}{9} \;\approx\; 0.444.
\end{equation}
The CPP metric is everywhere positive.  There is no radius at which
$g_{tt}^{\rm CPP} = 0$ above the Planck scale.

\subsection{The PSR horizon at the Planck scale}

The PSR formula $\PSR_{\rm eff}/l_P = 1/(1+k\Delta\SSV)$ approaches
zero only as $k\Delta\SSV \to \infty$, which requires
$r \to 0$.  The CP Exclusion Rule prevents any GP from being
occupied by more than one CP, imposing a physical lower bound:
\begin{equation}
  r_{\rm PSR} \;=\; \frac{l_P}{2} \;\approx\; 8.1\times 10^{-36}\ \text{m}.
  \label{eq:r_PSR}
\end{equation}

For a solar-mass object: $r_s \approx 2954$~m, $r_{\rm PSR} \approx
8\times 10^{-36}$~m, ratio $\approx 3.7\times 10^{38}$.

The physical picture: matter compressed beyond the Schwarzschild
radius does not form a horizon in CPP.  It continues to collapse until
the Planck-scale core is reached, at which point CP Exclusion
provides the hard floor.

\subsection{Gravitational redshift}

The exact CPP gravitational redshift for a photon emitted at $r$ and
observed at $r \to \infty$:
\begin{equation}
  1 + z_{\rm CPP} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{g_{tt}^{\rm CPP}(r)}}
  \;=\; 1 + \frac{r_s}{2r}.
  \label{eq:redshift}
\end{equation}

In GR: $1+z_{\rm GR} = (1-r_s/r)^{-1/2}$.

Both agree at large $r$.  At $r = r_s$: CPP gives $1+z = 3/2$
(finite), GR gives $1+z \to \infty$ (infinite redshift at horizon).

\section{The CPP Field Equation (Open Problem)}
\label{sec:field_eq}
% ==================================================================

This is the central open problem of the paper and of the CPP
gravitational program.

\subsection{What we have}

The weak-field companion and this paper take the SSV field
$\Delta\SSV(r)$ as \emph{given} by the mass distribution and
derive the metric from it.  This is analogous to solving for
the gravitational potential of a given source in Newtonian gravity
--- it works perfectly in the static, spherically symmetric case.

\subsection{What full GR requires}

General relativity's power comes from its \emph{field equation}:
$G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}/c^4$.  This governs how
mass-energy sources curvature and how curvature feeds back on
mass-energy propagation --- the self-consistent closed equation.

The CPP equivalent must express:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item How the LSP broadcast from a CP at position $\mathbf{x}$
  modifies the SSV field at all other positions.
\item How the modified SSV (and hence PSR contraction) feeds back
  on the geodesic followed by the CP itself.
\item How the discrete-to-continuum limit of this feedback
  recovers Einstein's field equations in the appropriate regime.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The CPP feedback loop (qualitative)}

From the Newtonian gravity companion:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Mass-energy $\to$ gravitational SSV broadcast.
\item SSV modifies local PSR: $\PSR_{\rm eff} = l_P/(1+k\Delta\SSV)$.
\item Modified PSR contracts the effective 600-cell lattice spacing.
\item Modified lattice changes how SSV from \emph{other} sources propagates.
\end{itemize}

This feedback loop is the CPP analog of spacetime curvature.

\subsection{Conjecture: the CPP field equation}

The self-consistent equation for the SSV field sourced by a
stress-energy distribution $T_{\mu\nu}$ is conjectured to be:

\begin{equation}
  \nabla^2_{\rm eff}\,\Delta\SSV(\mathbf{x})
  \;+\; k\,|\nabla\Delta\SSV|^2
  \;=\; -\frac{T_{00}}{l_P^2\,c^2},
  \label{eq:field_eq_conjecture}
\end{equation}

where $\nabla_{\rm eff}$ is the Laplacian on the PSR-modified lattice
and the $k|\nabla\Delta\SSV|^2$ term is the self-interaction of the
SSV field (the CPP analog of the nonlinear terms in the Einstein equations).

\begin{remark}[Status]
Equation~\eqref{eq:field_eq_conjecture} is a conjecture, not a derived
result.  Establishing it rigorously from the 600-cell broadcast rules
is the primary open problem of the CPP gravitational program.
Its linearisation recovers the Poisson equation for Newtonian gravity
(companion~5).  Whether its exact form recovers the Einstein field
equations is the question that determines whether CPP is a derivation
of GR or a distinct theory that agrees with GR in a specific limit.
\end{remark}
